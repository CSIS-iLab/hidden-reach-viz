Bejucal
22° 56' 54.5"N 82° 21' 46.29"W
Nestled in the hills overlooking Cuba’s capital city of Havana, the Bejucal SIGINT Facility is the country’s largest active SIGINT base. The facility gained notoriety for serving as a major storage facility for Soviet nuclear weapons during the 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis.

Bejucal has repeatedly been referenced as a site of potential Chinese intelligence activity going back decades, including in English and Spanish-language media reporting, congressional testimony, and unclassified government documents. It even featured in the 2016 U.S. presidential debate, when Florida Senator Marco Rubio called on Havana to “[kick] out this Chinese listening station in Bejucal.”

Satellite imagery reveals what is indeed a significant active military intelligence facility that has undergone major upgrades over the past decade. The secured facility features at least five distinct sections dedicated to different SIGINT mission-sets.


Wajay
23° 0'27.63"N 82°25'13.48"W
Less than 10 kilometers north of Bejucal lies a smaller facility called Wajay. The presence of security fencing and two guard posts strongly suggests that the site is intended for military or intelligence purposes.

The site has gradually expanded over the past 20 years, growing from just one antenna and several small buildings in 2002, to a more robust facility today. It now hosts 12 antennas of various sizes and orientations, significant operations and support facilities, and even a small solar farm. Unverified reports claim that China played a role either in the site’s construction or modernization.

No dish antennas are visible at the site, suggesting its purpose is likely primarily signal interception and transmission. However, the variety of antennas present is a clear indication that Wajay is responsible for a reasonably complex SIGINT mission-set.


Calabazar
23° 0'48.87"N 82°19'58.91"W
Close by, a small Cuban military facility bears the hallmarks of another SIGINT site. Over a dozen dish antennas of varying sizes are scattered throughout the secured facility, alongside two visible pole antenna arrays. As with most military intelligence installations, the number, location, and orientation of the antennas has shifted considerably over time, likely adjusting to changes in the site’s mission-set. The newest dish was installed in 2016.


Santiago de Cuba
20° 0'19.16"N 75°44'48.49"W
Numerous unverifiable sources assert that China has operated a large antenna field in the country’s far eastern province of Santiago de Cuba since 1999. Access to such a facility would provide China with a highly strategic vantage point over Naval Station Guantanamo Bay, one of the most important U.S. military bases in the region.

A facility matching this description could not be located through an extensive search of open-source records and over 2,900 square kilometers of satellite imagery across the province conducted by CSIS.

However, analysis by CSIS did reveal a significant new SIGINT collection site under construction just east of the city of Santiago de Cuba. The structure, which began construction in 2021, appears to be a large circularly disposed antenna array (CDAA), nearly 200 meters in diameter.

Sites like these were a staple of SIGINT collection during the Cold War. As hostilities between the two superpowers faded and new technologies emerged, Russia and the United States decommissioned or abandoned most of their CDAA facilities.3 China, however, has been actively building major new CDAA facilities, including on its militarized outposts on Mischief Reef and Subi Reef in the South China Sea.
